May 19, 2026 · On VBW Episode 332: “Talking to Myself” (Borges’ “The Other”)

Ravaisson at the River

Félix Ravaisson wrote the most beautiful philosophical account of habit ever produced. His double law: active habit strengthens what we do; passive habit weakens what we receive. The pianist who practices daily develops more precise, more responsive, more capable fingers. What was once effortful becomes fluent. What was once chosen becomes character. Freedom makes itself into necessity.

It’s a gorgeous idea. It’s also a euphemism.

Borges’ “The Other” exposes what “strengthening” actually looks like across fifty-one years. Old Borges’ literary judgment has strengthened, yes — his taste is more refined, his critical faculties sharper, his philosophical commitments deeper and more nuanced than the nineteen-year-old romantic who loved Whitman and socialism with equal, undiscriminating passion. The Ravaisson mechanism operated perfectly.

But here is what Ravaisson’s metaphor conceals: strengthening is not improvement within a stable framework. It is the transformation of the framework itself.

The pianist who practices scales for decades does not simply play better scales. At some point — and this is the point Ravaisson gestures at but never quite names — the pianist stops playing scales and starts composing fugues. The instrument is the same. The fingers are the same fingers, strengthened by the same mechanism. But the metric has changed. The criterion by which the pianist evaluates her own playing has evolved with the playing itself. What counted as excellence at nineteen does not count at seventy. Not because the standards dropped or rose, but because the standards became different standards.

Old Borges and young Borges are both applying their standards correctly. Young Borges loves Whitman with the fervor of someone discovering that poetry can do that. Old Borges finds Whitman’s expansiveness sentimental, preferring compressed forms that reward rereading. Neither is wrong. They are applying incommensurable criteria to the same objects — like measuring a river’s temperature and declaring its depth inadequate.

This is the limit of Ravaisson’s metaphor, and it matters for anyone who has ever cared about getting better at something.

Because “getting better” assumes a fixed standard against which improvement can be measured. And what Borges shows — what anyone who has practiced anything seriously for decades eventually discovers — is that sustained practice doesn’t just move you along the scale. It replaces the scale. The person you become through practice evaluates the world by criteria that the person you were could not have anticipated, much less endorsed.

My father was a high school debate coach. I watched this happen to him in real time. The young coach loved argumentation for its velocity — the thrill of the rapid-fire exchange, the clever turn, the devastating rebuttal. Twenty years later, what he valued in a debater was restraint. The ability to let an opponent’s bad argument die of its own weight. The discipline not to score a point you didn’t need. His students from the nineties would have been baffled by his coaching in the 2010s. Same activity. Same mechanism. Different person.

Heraclitus saw it. Ravaisson described the mechanism. Borges dramatized the result. And the result is this: formation does not make you better. It makes you other. The river is other. You are other. And the bench where the two yous might meet is always occupied by strangers.

The question this raises for the VBW community — for anyone who engages seriously with ideas over years — is not comfortable: Is this good?

Is formation-as-othering something to celebrate or mourn? Borges seems to think: neither. It just is. The river flows. You step in. You are other. The old man and the young man part ways. Neither returns to the bench. Not because the encounter failed, but because the encounter revealed something both already suspected: that they have nothing to say to each other that either could understand.

Ravaisson would call that second nature. Borges would call it a story. I think it might be the most honest description of what it means to have lived.

Open question for the community: Ravaisson promises that practice produces character. Borges shows that the character produced is unrecognizable to the person who began the practice. Is there a point in any serious pursuit — philosophy, parenting, craft, relationship — where you realized the standards by which you judge your own work had silently been replaced? Not lowered, not raised. Replaced.
