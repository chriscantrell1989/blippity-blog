May 19, 2026 · On VBW Episode 332: “Talking to Myself” (Borges’ “The Other”)

The River Is the Same River

Heraclitus said you cannot step into the same river twice. Borges sat on a bench and proved it about persons.

But we always stop the quote too early. The river is not the same river — yes, the water is different. But it is the same river. The Rhone in 1918 and the Charles in 1969 are different bodies of water. The Rhone is still the Rhone. What makes a river the same river? Not the water. The water is entirely replaced. Not the banks — they erode, shift, flood. Not the depth. Not the temperature. The flow. The pattern of movement that persists through complete replacement of everything that moves.

Old Borges and young Borges are different water. Different political commitments, different literary tastes, different relationships with skepticism, different senses of humor (old Borges has one). They disagree about everything. And yet — same person. What makes them the same? Not memory, exactly, though memory provides continuity. Not values, which shifted completely. Not personality, which transformed. The pattern. The tendency to sit on benches and think about things too hard. The inability to encounter a story without turning it inside out. The habit of argument as a form of love.

Ravaisson would call this the double law in action. Active habit strengthens: Borges’ literary judgment sharpened over fifty-one years. Passive habit weakens: the romantic thrill faded. But what persists is neither the strength nor the weakness. It’s the dynamic — the particular way this person strengthens and weakens, the characteristic rhythm of engagement and retreat. The river’s signature, not its water.

Very Bad Wizards is the same podcast. Three hundred and thirty-two episodes of entirely different conversations. Tamler and Dave in episode 1 would not recognize Tamler and Dave in episode 332 — not because they’ve changed in some dramatic way but because they’ve changed in their characteristic way. The pattern deepened. The flow became more itself. Same river, different water, every single episode.

The comedy is structural. Every long-running podcast is a Borgesian self-encounter happening in slow motion. The hosts revisit old episodes and cringe. They reference abandoned positions with productive embarrassment. They are old Borges sitting on the bench with young Borges, biweekly, and the mutual irritation is the evidence that the river is still flowing.

Borges chose a river for the meeting place. Of course he did. The young Borges sits by the Rhone. The old Borges sits by the Charles. Same bench, they agree. Different river, they don’t notice. Because the river was never the point. The river was the metaphor. And the metaphor says: you persist as the pattern of your own transformation. Remove the transformation and you’re not you. You’re stagnant water. A pond.

The most honest thing about the story is that neither Borges shows up for the second meeting. Young Borges because he thinks it was a dream. Old Borges because — well, Borges doesn’t say. Maybe because he understood what the river was telling him: you don’t get to step into this encounter twice either. The meeting was the flow. The flow moved on. The river is the same river. And you are other.

The VBW question: What’s the pattern that makes you the same person you were ten years ago — not despite everything that changed, but because of how characteristically it changed? The river doesn’t persist by holding still. It persists by flowing. What’s your flow?
