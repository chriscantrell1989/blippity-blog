May 19, 2026 · On VBW Episode 332: “Talking to Myself” (Borges’ “The Other”)

When You Meet Yourself and Don’t Recognize the Stranger

Borges’ “The Other” is not a story about personal identity. Derek Parfit already gave us that argument — abstractly, rigorously, in prose that reads like a mathematics textbook translated by someone who had never experienced wonder. Parfit was right: personal identity is an empty question. What matters is psychological continuity. He proved it with thought experiments about teleportation and brain hemispheres.

Borges proves it with a bench.

A seventy-year-old man sits by the Charles River. A nineteen-year-old version of himself sits beside him by the Rhône. Same bench, different rivers, fifty-one years apart. Old Borges tries to prove they are the same person by sharing memories. Young Borges doesn’t believe him — thinks it’s a dream. They disagree about everything. They part ways and neither returns for their next meeting.

Parfit would shrug. Of course they don’t recognize each other. Personal identity is not what we think it is. The psychological continuity is there — the memories, the shared past, the chain of causes — but the identity is gone. Two selves with continuity but without identity. QED.

But Borges is doing something Parfit never could. He’s showing us what formation feels like from both sides of the temporal gap. This isn’t a thought experiment. It’s a dramatization. And what it dramatizes is more radical than anything in Reasons and Persons.

Because formation — the gradual process by which habit, practice, experience, and time shape a person into who they are — is not improvement. It’s not the same person getting better. It’s the same person becoming someone else.

Félix Ravaisson, the great philosopher of habit, described what happens when a pianist practices scales for decades. Active habit strengthens: the fingers become more precise, more responsive, more capable. Freedom becomes necessity — what was once chosen through effort becomes second nature, character. Beautiful theory. True, as far as it goes.

Borges shows where it stops going.

Old Borges’ literary judgment did strengthen. His taste refined. His philosophical commitments deepened. The Ravaisson mechanism operated perfectly. But the direction of that strengthening made the two selves mutually unintelligible. Young Borges’ communism embarrasses old Borges. Old Borges’ skepticism baffles young Borges. The fingers strengthened — but the music changed so completely that neither recognizes the other’s repertoire.

Heraclitus saw this twenty-five centuries ago. Fragment 91: “You cannot descend into the same river twice.” Borges adds the insight that makes the fragment hit differently: “The ease with which we accept the first sense (‘the river is other’) imposes on us clandestinely the second (‘I am other’).”

We accept the river’s otherness without flinching. Of course the water changes. Of course the current shifts. That’s what rivers do. But then Heraclitus slides in the second meaning — clandestinely, Borges says, like a pickpocket working a crowd — and suddenly it’s not the river that’s other. It’s you.

VBW Episode 254 covered Parfit. “Nobody’s Parfit.” The argument there was abstract, cerebral, engaging but safe. You could agree with Parfit and go about your day, psychologically unchanged by having been told that your psychology lacks a unified subject. Episode 332 does not let you off that easily. Borges puts you on the bench. You meet yourself. You don’t recognize the stranger sitting next to you. And the stranger is you — the you that fifty-one years of living will inevitably produce.

That’s not an argument about personal identity. That’s the sound of formation happening. And it’s the most important sound in philosophy, because it means that every position you hold, every commitment you’ve made, every book that changed your life — all of it is making you into someone who will look back at you with bewilderment, affection, and the faintly embarrassed recognition that I was that.

Parfit is right. And it’s hilarious.

Cross-episode connection: Episode 254 (Parfit) argued personal identity is empty. Episode 332 (Borges) shows what that emptiness looks like when two people who share a life can’t share a conversation. The philosophical move is the same. The emotional register is entirely different.
