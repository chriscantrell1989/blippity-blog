May 19, 2026 · On VBW Episode 332: “Talking to Myself” (Borges’ “The Other”)

Of Course They Disagreed About Literature

The funniest moment in Borges’ “The Other” is not the metaphysical conceit of a man meeting his younger self by a river. It’s not the Heraclitean vertigo or the dream-reality collapse. It’s that the two Borgeses — separated by fifty-one years, sharing a bench across two continents and two centuries — argue about books.

Of course they do.

Every reader over forty has met their nineteen-year-old self in a used bookstore and cringed. The books you loved at nineteen — the ones that felt like they were written for you personally, like the author had reached across time and grabbed you by the collar — look different at seventy. Not wrong. Not naive, exactly. Just… from another country. A country you lived in, that you can describe perfectly, but that you can never visit again.

Old Borges finds young Borges’ enthusiasms embarrassing. Young Borges finds old Borges’ refinement boring. They are both absolutely right. And the mutual irritation is comedy in its purest philosophical form.

Bergson argued that laughter arises when the mechanical is encrusted on the living. We laugh when a person behaves like a thing — when the rigid overlays the supple, when habit replaces responsiveness. What Borges gives us is the temporal version: each self sees the other as the mechanical one. Each self sees the other as stuck — stuck in romanticism, stuck in skepticism, stuck in the assumptions of their era. The living person is always the one doing the laughing.

But here’s the Borgesian twist: both of them are the living one. Both of them are right. Which means both of them are also wrong — wrong about themselves, specifically. Each is as stuck as the other. Each is as alive as the other. The comedy is perfectly symmetrical, and that symmetry is the Heraclitean insight. The river flows in both directions at once — young toward old, old toward young — and neither stream can see itself flowing.

Tamler and Dave have been living this for years. They revisit old episodes and wince. They quote earlier positions they’ve since abandoned with exactly the mix of affection and mortification that old Borges brings to young Borges’ literary taste. The podcast is old-Borges-meets-young-Borges, biweekly. Every episode is a self-encounter across time.

Episode 332 gives them a story about that encounter. The meta-level is the joke. And the joke is that we all think we’re the living one — the one who finally understands, who has arrived at the real position, who has shed the mechanical husk of earlier naivety. We never have. We never will. Because the person we’ll be in twenty years will look back at us with the same productive embarrassment we bring to our twenty-year-old selves.

That’s not a tragedy. It’s a comedy. And it might be the most honest thing philosophy can say about personal identity: you will always be someone else’s punchline. Especially your own.

The VBW question: What’s a position you held five years ago that you now find embarrassing — but that you know you had to hold in order to get where you are now? The embarrassment is the evidence that formation happened. The fact that you can’t go back is the punchline.
