May 19, 2026 · On VBW Episodes 331 & 332: Crito & Borges’ “The Other”

Socrates’ Ghost at the River

Put Episodes 331 and 332 next to each other. Something emerges that neither episode says alone.

Episode 331: Socrates in the Crito, two days before hemlock. His oldest friend begs him to escape. Socrates refuses — not from resignation but from fidelity. Decades of dialectical practice have formed a character that cannot choose flight. Ravaisson’s formula made flesh: freedom has become necessity. What was once philosophical commitment has hardened into something closer to instinct. He doesn’t weigh the arguments anymore. He is the arguments.

Episode 332: Borges meets his younger self by a river. Fifty-one years of formation between them. They share memories but not a single judgment. The old man’s literary taste embarrasses the young man. The young man’s political convictions embarrass the old. They are the same person the way the Charles and the Rhône are the same river — which is to say, they aren’t.

Now imagine Socrates at that bench.

Not the Socrates of the Crito — not the seventy-year-old gadfly who has spent a lifetime perfecting the art of making powerful people uncomfortable. Imagine the young Socrates. The stonemason’s son who had not yet heard the Oracle at Delphi, had not yet spent decades in the agora asking questions that made people want to kill him, had not yet sat in a cell choosing death over incoherence.

If that young Socrates met the old one on a bench by the Ilissos, would he understand the choice?

Almost certainly not.

The young Socrates would see a man choosing to die for principles the young Socrates hasn’t formed yet. He would hear arguments that depend on thirty years of dialectical habit he hasn’t built. He would look at the old man’s calm in the face of execution and see not wisdom but madness — or at best, a dream. Young Borges’ response to old Borges: this isn’t real.

This is what makes Episodes 331 and 332 a pair. The Crito shows us formation’s triumph: character so deeply practiced that it holds under the ultimate pressure. “The Other” shows us formation’s cost: character so deeply practiced that it becomes unrecognizable to the person it grew out of.

Same process. Same mechanism. Ravaisson’s double law operating in both directions at once.

Socrates could not have explained his choice to his younger self. Not because the argument is bad — the argument is extraordinary. But because the argument only makes sense to someone who has spent decades inside the practice that produced it. The pre-dialectical Socrates doesn’t have the ears to hear it. He hasn’t been formed yet. He’s still standing on the other bank of the river, and the water between them is thirty years wide.

Formation is real. This is Ravaisson’s lesson, and neither Socrates nor Borges contradicts it. The habit deepens. The character forms. The practice produces a person who could not have been produced any other way.

But formation produces someone new. This is Borges’ lesson, and the Crito confirms it from the opposite direction. The Socrates who stays is not a better version of the young stonemason. He is someone else. Someone the stonemason would have dismissed as a dream — or followed out of curiosity rather than comprehension.

You can’t go back. Not because the past is lost, but because there’s no one to go back to. The person you were couldn’t understand the person you’ve become, and the person you’ve become can’t undo the formation that made comprehension impossible. That’s not a tragedy. It’s the structure of growth. And it’s the structure of the VBW archive — 332 episodes of two hosts becoming people their earlier selves wouldn’t recognize, one conversation at a time.

The two-episode thesis: Episode 331 asks: can formation hold in solitude? (Yes — Socrates stays.) Episode 332 asks: can the formed self recognize the unformed? (No — Borges fails.) Together they describe the full arc of what practice does to a person: it makes you strong enough to die for your convictions, and strange enough that the person who gave you those convictions wouldn’t recognize you.
