May 19, 2026 · On VBW Episode 332: “Talking to Myself” (Borges’ “The Other”)

The Asymmetry of Self-Encounter

Here is the detail in “The Other” that unsettles me most.

Old Borges thinks the encounter is real. Young Borges thinks it’s a dream.

Not a disagreement about books or politics. A disagreement about ontology. About whether what is happening is happening at all. One party to the encounter grants it reality. The other dismisses it as the residue of sleep — vivid, strange, worth mentioning to friends in the morning, but not real. Not something that makes claims on you. Not something you need to take seriously.

This is not a symmetrical problem.

Hannah Arendt argued that action — meaningful human action, the kind that constitutes a life rather than merely extending it — requires the “space of appearance.” You must appear to others. Others must appear to you. The space between you is where meaning happens. Without it, action collapses into work: mere fabrication, mere production, mere repetition. You can build a table alone. You cannot act alone. Action requires plurality.

Arendt’s nightmare was appearing to no one — the totalitarian condition in which public space is destroyed and each person is sealed inside their own isolation. But Borges describes something subtler and, I think, worse: appearing to someone who denies that you are appearing.

Old Borges appears to young Borges. He sits on the bench, shares memories, tries to prove identity. He appears — in the full Arendtian sense, presenting himself to another person in the shared world. But young Borges treats the entire encounter as a dream. The space of appearance exists for old Borges and not for young Borges. One party is in the world. The other party is in a dream about the world. They share a bench but not a reality.

This is worse than Arendt’s no-one. If you appear to no one, at least the absence is clear. You know you’re alone. You can adjust. But if you appear to someone who experiences your appearance as non-real — who hears you, sees you, argues with you, and then files the entire encounter under “interesting dream I had once” — then the space of appearance is not merely absent. It is counterfeit. It mimics reality from your side while dissolving into unreality from theirs.

My mother came home from the hospital and told stories about patients. She had spent twelve hours in a world that was, for her, the most real thing she had ever experienced. She told her family about it. We heard her. We nodded. We asked follow-up questions. But did we treat those stories as real — as making claims on us, as demanding response? Or did we treat them as the dreamlike residue of a world we couldn’t enter? The asymmetry between practitioner and observer is built into every profession. Borges makes it visible.

The asymmetry is temporal. The formed self recognizes the unformed self — old Borges sees young Borges clearly, can predict his enthusiasms, understands his limitations. But the unformed self cannot recognize the formed self. Young Borges doesn’t have the framework to interpret old Borges’ refinement as refinement. He sees it as diminishment, as a failure of passion, as the boring residue of a life that traded fire for competence.

Recognition runs one way. Like time.

The formed self cannot force the unformed self to see. Old Borges cannot make young Borges understand what fifty-one years of formation produced, because understanding would itself require the formation that young Borges hasn’t yet undergone. The argument is circular — and the circularity is not a logical flaw. It’s the structure of formation itself. You cannot understand what practice does until practice has done it. By then, you can no longer explain it to the person you were.

This might be the deepest thing Borges ever wrote. Not a puzzle about identity or time. A portrait of the fundamental loneliness of having changed — the loneliness of knowing something that your own past self would dismiss as a dream.

The Arendt question: What is worse — appearing to no one (isolation), or appearing to someone who treats your appearance as non-real (dream-dismissal)? Borges suggests the second. At least in isolation you know where you stand.
